Service fit

When virtual care is a strong fit.

Virtual support is real care, but it is not the right first format for every family. The fastest way to build trust is to say that clearly.

Good fit

  • ✓Caregivers want coaching, structure, and practical strategies they can use during everyday routines.
  • ✓The child can tolerate short on-screen interaction, or the caregiver can serve as the in-person support during sessions.
  • ✓The main goals are communication, behavior support, routines, flexibility, parent training, and focused skill building.
  • ✓The family wants faster access than waiting months for a traditional in-home slot.
  • ✓The plan is a focused model, not a giant promise of every service for every case.

May need hybrid or in-person first

  • !Very young children who need hands-on support throughout the session and do not yet benefit from caregiver-mediated work.
  • !Cases with significant aggression, elopement, or safety concerns that need in-person environmental control.
  • !Families expecting a heavy technician-hours model right away rather than focused BCBA/LBA-guided care.
  • !Situations where licensure, payer contracting, or clinical ownership structure is not yet properly set up.
  • !Families who need a higher-acuity care model than a virtual-first startup can safely deliver on day one.
The process

How Harbor Path can make this feel simple.

The cleaner model is a fast virtual front door, then clinical delivery through the right New York setup. That is easier to sell and easier to operate.

01

Intro call

Start with a short phone or video conversation to understand the family’s goals, urgency, and whether virtual support is a good fit.

02

Clinical fit review

Confirm diagnosis, service goals, payment path, and what part Harbor Path handles versus what the licensed clinical partner handles.

03

Assessment & plan

Create a focused treatment plan around the few goals that matter most instead of overwhelming the family with a giant program.

04

Start support

Begin recurring virtual sessions, caregiver coaching, reporting, and next-step coordination from a single simple care path.

FAQ

Questions families will ask right away.

A page like this converts better when you answer the obvious concerns before the family has to ask.

Is virtual ABA right for every child?

No. Some families are a great fit for virtual caregiver coaching or focused therapy, while others need a hybrid or in-person-first model. Saying that clearly builds trust.

Do you accept insurance?

Only say yes when it is really true. Until payer contracts and the clinical structure are live, use wording like “ask about current payment options and partner-based insurance pathways.”

Do you work outside NYC?

Yes, if your licensed clinical setup supports telepractice across New York. Until you are truly multi-state, avoid saying “everywhere” on the public page.

Will this replace in-person services?

Sometimes yes, sometimes no. The best framing is that virtual support can be the right first step, the full model for some families, or part of a broader care plan.
